Shivaji Bhosale, (February 1630 â€“ 3 April 1680), also referred to as Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj, was an Indian ruler and a member of the Bhosle Maratha clan. Shivaji carved out his own independent kingdom from the declining Adilshahi sultanate of Bijapur which formed the genesis of the Maratha Empire. In 1674, he was formally crowned the Chhatrapati of his realm at Raigad Fort.

Below is a brief timeline of Maharaja's life.

A Brief Timeline

19 February 1630

Born on Shivneri Fort, near Pune. His mother Jijabai named him "Shivaji" in honour of goddess Shivai, deity of the fort.

In 1643-47

Overran the hill forts of Kondana, Torana and Raigad.

In 1656

Conquered Javli from Chandra Rao More.

In 1657

Came into conflict with Moghuls for the first time after he made raids into Ahmednagar.

In 1659

Killed Afzal Khan of Bijapur who was assigned the task to defeat Shivaji, and won the battle of Pratapgad.

In 1660

Trapped in the fort of Panhala, escapes after the battle of Pawan Khind, Moghul Governor Shaista Khan occupied in Pune.

In 1663

Shivaji attaked and wounded Shaista Khan.

In 1665

Treaty of Purandar - Shivaji agreed to give up 23 of his forts.

In 1666

Shivaji summoned to Agra by Aurangzeb, put under house arrest, but escaped.

In 1670

Launched an offensive against the Mughals, attacked Surat for the second time.

In 1674

Crowned King of Maratha Swaraj at Raigad fort and assumed the title "Maharaja Chhatrapati".

In 1680

Died after an illness.

Over the course of his life, Shivaji engaged in both alliances and hostilities with the Mughal Empire, the Sultanate of Golkonda, Sultanate of Bijapur and the European colonial powers. Shivaji's military forces expanded the Maratha sphere of influence, capturing and building forts, and forming a Maratha navy. Shivaji established a competent and progressive civil rule with well-structured administrative organisations. He revived ancient Hindu political traditions, court conventions and promoted the usage of the Marathi and Sanskrit languages, replacing Persian in court and administration.

Shivaji's legacy was to vary by observer and time, but nearly two centuries after his death, he began to take on increased importance with the emergence of the Indian independence movement, as many Indian nationalists elevated him as a proto-nationalist and hero of the Hindus.
